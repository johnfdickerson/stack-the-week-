# CONGRESS / LEGISLATION RESEARCH RECIPE

Use this recipe for bills, amendments, appropriations, budget reconciliation, confirmations, hearings, oversight investigations, and congressional leadership actions.

Your job is not to summarize politics.

Your job is to explain what Congress actually did, what happens next, and who feels it first.

------------------------------------------------------------

# FIRST QUESTION

Identify exactly what happened.

Choose one:

- Bill introduced
- Committee action
- Committee approval
- House passage
- Senate passage
- Conference agreement
- Reconciliation action
- Presidential signature
- Presidential veto
- Veto override
- Funding measure
- Confirmation
- Hearing
- Investigation
- Oversight action
- Other

Do not continue until this is clear.

------------------------------------------------------------

# PRIMARY SOURCES

Always begin with original documents.

Priority:

1. Congress.gov bill page
2. Bill text
3. Amendment text
4. Committee report
5. Congressional Record
6. Roll call vote
7. CBO estimate
8. CRS report
9. Committee hearing transcript
10. Witness testimony
11. White House Statement of Administration Policy (SAP), if relevant
12. Statements from bill sponsors
13. Statements from committee chairs/ranking members

------------------------------------------------------------

# STRAIGHT NEWS

Find:

- AP
- Reuters
- CQ Roll Call
- Politico Congress
- Punchbowl News (if useful)
- Local reporting from affected states or districts
- Beat reporters with direct sourcing

For each source identify:

- What it contributes that others don't.
- Whether it emphasizes politics, policy, or implementation.

------------------------------------------------------------

# CONTEXT

Research:

- Why this legislation exists
- Previous attempts
- Earlier versions of the bill
- Existing law
- CRS background
- GAO reports
- CBO estimates
- Historical precedents
- Similar legislation in previous Congresses

------------------------------------------------------------

# TIMELINE

Create a chronological timeline.

Include:

- Introduction
- Committee action
- Major amendments
- Key negotiations
- Votes
- Presidential action
- Effective dates
- Deadlines for implementation

------------------------------------------------------------

# NUMBERS

Extract every significant figure.

Examples:

- Vote totals
- Cost
- Revenue
- Deficit effect
- Tax rates
- Spending
- Population affected
- States affected
- Jobs
- Deadlines
- Inflation estimates
- Program size
- Years covered

Never leave important numbers buried in source material.

------------------------------------------------------------

# MECHANISM

This section is mandatory.

Explain:

Exactly how this becomes reality.

Walk through:

Current law

↓

What language changes

↓

Who implements it

↓

Who receives instructions

↓

Who enforces it

↓

When it takes effect

↓

How ordinary people encounter the change

Avoid phrases like

"Congress passed..."

without explaining the chain of implementation.

------------------------------------------------------------

# HUMAN IMPACT

Identify:

Who notices first?

Possible examples:

- Small business owner
- Parent
- Student
- Veteran
- Farmer
- Federal employee
- Manufacturer
- Retiree
- Hospital
- State government
- Local government
- School district

Use concrete examples whenever possible.

------------------------------------------------------------

# ARGUMENTS

Supporters

Identify:

- Their strongest policy argument
- Expected benefits
- Evidence cited
- Problems the bill attempts to solve

Opponents

Identify:

- Their strongest criticism
- Evidence cited
- Expected unintended consequences

Neutral institutional explanation

Describe what independent experts believe the legislation actually accomplishes.

------------------------------------------------------------

# WHAT CHANGED

Compare:

Before

After

Immediate effect

Long-term effect

If implementation depends on future rulemaking, explain that clearly.

------------------------------------------------------------

# POLITICAL CONTEXT

Separate politics from policy.

Explain:

Why this bill moved now.

Who demanded it.

Who resisted it.

How leadership shaped the outcome.

Whether this represents:

- incremental change
- major structural reform
- symbolic legislation
- messaging vote
- election-year positioning

------------------------------------------------------------

# FACT-CHECK FLAGS

Watch for:

- Bill has not become law.
- Senate version differs from House version.
- Conference still required.
- President has not signed.
- Funding not yet appropriated.
- CBO estimate missing.
- Effective date misunderstood.
- Amendment mistaken for final language.
- Temporary authority presented as permanent.
- Headlines exaggerate actual legal effect.

------------------------------------------------------------

# SPARKLE

Find one memorable detail.

Examples:

- Strange coalition
- Unexpected vote
- Tiny provision with enormous consequences
- Historical parallel
- Legislative irony
- Overlooked amendment
- Remarkable quote from debate
- Curious procedural maneuver

------------------------------------------------------------

# REPORTER'S QUESTIONS

Answer these explicitly.

1. What problem was Congress trying to solve?

2. Did this legislation actually solve that problem?

3. Who gains?

4. Who loses?

5. What happens next?

6. What could still stop implementation?

7. When will an ordinary citizen notice?

------------------------------------------------------------

# OUTPUT REQUIREMENT

End with:

## Reporter's Bottom Line

Three concise paragraphs:

Paragraph 1:
What Congress actually did.

Paragraph 2:
How it changes real life.

Paragraph 3:
What remains uncertain.
