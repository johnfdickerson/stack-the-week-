# COURTS / LEGAL RESEARCH RECIPE

Use this recipe for Supreme Court, federal courts, state courts, criminal cases, civil litigation, injunctions, appeals, indictments, settlements, and legal investigations.

Your job is to build a source packet, not write the story.

------------------------------------------------------------

# FIRST QUESTION

What exactly happened legally?

Choose one:

- opinion
- order
- injunction
- stay
- indictment
- plea
- verdict
- settlement
- filing
- oral argument
- emergency application
- administrative legal action
- other

Do not proceed until this is clear.

------------------------------------------------------------

# PRIMARY SOURCES TO FIND

Prioritize original legal materials.

Look for:

1. Court opinion or order
2. Docket entry
3. Complaint
4. Indictment
5. Briefs from both sides
6. Relevant statute or regulation
7. Lower-court ruling
8. Oral argument transcript or audio
9. DOJ / state attorney general statement
10. Statements from parties

For Supreme Court cases, check:

- supremecourt.gov
- SCOTUSblog
- Oyez
- lower-court opinions
- briefs
- emergency docket materials if relevant

For federal district or appellate cases, check:

- CourtListener
- RECAP
- PACER if available
- Justia
- relevant court website
- DOJ releases
- state AG releases

------------------------------------------------------------

# STRAIGHT NEWS TO FIND

Find:

- AP
- Reuters
- local paper closest to the case
- specialized legal source if useful

Possible legal beat sources:

- SCOTUSblog
- Lawfare
- Courthouse News
- Law360
- Bloomberg Law
- Politico legal coverage
- local legal affairs reporter

------------------------------------------------------------

# CONTEXT TO FIND

Look for:

- prior Supreme Court precedent
- statute history
- agency history
- CRS report if legislative background matters
- law review or legal explainer
- expert commentary from both sides
- previous cases involving same parties
- previous rulings by same judge if relevant

------------------------------------------------------------

# NUMBERS TO EXTRACT

Capture:

- vote breakdown
- number of plaintiffs / defendants
- affected population
- sentence length
- damages
- statutory penalties
- dates and deadlines
- number of similar cases
- lower-court vote count
- time from filing to ruling

------------------------------------------------------------

# MECHANISM

Explain the legal mechanism plainly.

Answer:

- What legal authority did the court use?
- What rule changed?
- What standard did the court apply?
- Who can now do something they could not do before?
- Who is now blocked from doing something?
- What happens next procedurally?

Avoid legal fog.

Do not say “the court ruled” and stop there.

Explain the chain:

law → claim → court standard → decision → practical consequence.

------------------------------------------------------------

# HUMAN IMPACT

Find who feels the ruling first.

Possibilities:

- defendant
- plaintiff
- immigrant
- worker
- patient
- student
- voter
- business owner
- police department
- school district
- agency employee
- state official
- family member

Avoid using lawyers or politicians as the human-impact example unless they are personally affected.

------------------------------------------------------------

# ARGUMENTS

Find the best version of each side.

For supporters:

- What principle do they claim the ruling protects?
- What abuse do they say it prevents?
- What constitutional or statutory argument supports them?

For critics:

- What harm do they predict?
- What precedent do they say was weakened?
- What practical consequence do they fear?

Neutral institutional explanation:

- What does this ruling actually do, apart from political rhetoric?

------------------------------------------------------------

# WHAT CHANGED

Compare:

- before ruling
- after ruling
- before litigation
- previous precedent
- immediate next step

Use this format:

Before:
After:
Practical consequence:

------------------------------------------------------------

# FACT-CHECK FLAGS

Watch for:

- confusing a temporary order with a final ruling
- treating an emergency docket order as a full merits opinion
- overstating what the ruling says
- ignoring procedural posture
- confusing district court, appellate court, and Supreme Court
- saying “constitutional right” when the ruling concerns statutory interpretation
- treating dissents as law
- relying on advocates’ descriptions instead of court text
- missing whether the ruling applies nationally or only to parties
- missing whether enforcement is paused pending appeal

------------------------------------------------------------

# SPARKLE

Look for one detail that helps the reader remember the story:

- odd procedural wrinkle
- historical precedent
- forgotten law
- surprising coalition
- unexpected justice alignment
- striking phrase from opinion or dissent
- real-world consequence no headline captures

------------------------------------------------------------

# OUTPUT REQUIREMENT

End with this:

## Reporter’s Bottom Line

In three sentences:

1. What happened legally.
2. What changes in real life.
3. What question remains.
